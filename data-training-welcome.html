Knowledge, Time, and the Responsibility of Science

We are not the first

Seventeen thousand years ago, in what is now southwestern France, someone painted animals on the walls of a cave. We do not know their name, their language, or their intentions with certainty. But we do know that they were not acting randomly. Sites such as Lascaux Cave provide some of the earliest material evidence that humans were externalizing knowledge, placing it outside the mind, making it visible, and therefore shareable. These images, often layered over time, do not suggest isolated acts. They suggest continuity, with marks added in relation to marks that were already there [1][2]. Whether these paintings explained hunting strategies, symbolic systems, or collective memory remains debated. What is less debated is their implication. Knowledge was no longer confined to individuals. It was being preserved, encountered again, and potentially built upon. This is where the story begins.

Knowledge as inheritance

Humans are not independent producers of knowledge. We are, as has been described, “the accumulated product of thousands of generations” [3]. What we know is inseparable from what has been known before us. In anthropology and cognitive science, this process is defined as cumulative culture, the gradual accumulation and refinement of knowledge across individuals and generations [1][2]. Cultural traits do not simply persist. They improve, stabilize, and extend over time. Through this process, human societies produce forms of knowledge that no individual could generate alone. This is not a metaphor. It is an empirical observation. From agriculture to modern computing, human achievements exceed individual cognitive limits because they are distributed across time. As Derex notes, the scientific and technological systems that structure contemporary life are “the result of many minds building on each other’s work across time” [1]. What appears as innovation is, in fact, accumulation. This accumulation follows what has been described as a “ratchet effect,” in which useful innovations are preserved and incrementally improved rather than lost [1][2]. Knowledge does not reset between generations. It moves forward because it is retained.

The deep history of accumulation

Archaeological evidence situates this process deep in human history. For long periods, early stone tools remained relatively stable, suggesting limited accumulation. Yet around 600,000 years ago, a clear shift becomes visible. Tool-making techniques grow significantly more complex, involving multi-step production processes and increasing standardization [4]. This transition is widely interpreted as evidence of enhanced social learning and transmission. As one study concludes, “by 600,000 years ago or so, hominin populations started relying on unusually complex technologies” [4]. The implication is profound. Knowledge was no longer being reinvented in each generation. It was being preserved, shared, and extended. This capacity distinguishes human societies. While other species can learn, humans build. Techniques are not merely repeated but refined across time, producing systems that no individual could reconstruct independently [4][5].

Writing and the durability of knowledge

The invention of writing transformed this process further. Around 3400 BCE, knowledge became materially durable. Clay tablets, papyrus scrolls, and later manuscripts allowed ideas to persist beyond the lifespan of their creators. Agricultural techniques, legal systems, astronomical observations, and medical knowledge could now be recorded, stored, and transmitted across generations. This did more than preserve knowledge. It changed its structure. Once written, ideas could be revisited, compared, corrected, and extended. Knowledge no longer depended on continuous oral transmission. It became anchored in durable records that could be accessed and built upon [1]. Medieval manuscript culture illustrates this continuity. Scribes copying texts were not merely preserving information, they were maintaining the conditions under which knowledge could continue to exist. Works such as Euclidean geometry or Galenic medicine survived not because they were fixed, but because they were transmitted and reinterpreted.

Printing and the expansion of access

The invention of the printing press introduced a new scale. With the technology associated with the Gutenberg Printing Press, texts could be reproduced in large numbers and distributed widely. Knowledge was no longer confined to singular manuscripts or local circulation. It could spread, be contested, and be integrated into new work at an unprecedented pace. Within two centuries, scientific journals emerged, such as Philosophical Transactions, formalizing the recording and dissemination of knowledge. Scientific work became part of an ongoing archive, designed not only to communicate results but to make them available for verification, critique, and extension. By this stage, the cumulative nature of science had become explicit. Modern scientific systems, from technological infrastructures to institutional organization, have become too complex to be produced by individuals alone [4]. They depend on sustained contributions across generations.

The digital scale of knowledge

The digital era extends this trajectory further, both in scale and in speed. Data, code, publications, and methods can now be shared globally with minimal friction. Repositories, databases, and collaborative platforms allow knowledge to circulate almost instantly. A dataset generated in one context can inform research in another. A piece of code can be reused, modified, and integrated into new systems. Examples such as GenBank demonstrate this clearly. Genetic data collected across studies becomes part of a shared resource, continuously expanded and reinterpreted. Open-source software and collaborative platforms further illustrate that knowledge production is increasingly distributed. Even the laboratory notebook, once private, can now be archived and made accessible, contributing to a broader infrastructure of shared knowledge [6]. Across all these developments, the principle remains unchanged. Future innovation depends on access to past innovation.

The ongoing curse

And yet, despite this long history of accumulation, there is a recurring failure. The infrastructure for sharing exists, but practices do not always follow. Data remain inaccessible. Methods are insufficiently documented. Results cannot be reproduced. When this happens, the continuity of knowledge is interrupted. This is not simply an unpleasant inefficiency, but it is an actual structural break. If knowledge cannot be accessed, understood, or reused, it cannot participate in cumulative culture. Then, it cannot be built upon. In practical terms, it ceases to function as knowledge within the broader system. This is the ongoing curse. Not the absence of knowledge, but the failure to carry it forward. Not ignorance, but discontinuity. This responsibility is not abstract. Knowledge can only be transmitted if it remains interpretable, accessible, and reusable. Data that are undocumented, inaccessible, or irreproducible interrupt this continuity. In such cases, knowledge becomes effectively lost, not because it disappears, but because it cannot be used. Preservation is not enough. What must be preserved is usability.

Science as a collective responsibility

The idea that scientific knowledge is collective rather than individual is increasingly recognized in policy. The UNESCO Recommendation on Open Science (2021) defines science as a global public good, emphasizing that its benefits should be accessible to all [7]. This is not a new principle. It is a recognition of an old reality. Knowledge has always been inherited. What changes is how deliberately we preserve and share it. Open science is not simply a technical or institutional shift. It is the continuation of a long human process. It formalizes the responsibility to ensure that knowledge remains available for future generations. Failure to do so has consequences. Historical evidence shows that when knowledge is not transmitted, it can be lost. Techniques disappear, insights gone and entire domains of understanding can vanish [1].

Do you realize what you owe?

The ethical question is not new, but it is now unavoidable. Across time, knowledge has moved forward through reciprocity. Each generation has benefited from what was preserved by those before it, from cave markings to manuscripts, from printed books to digital archives. What we inherit was not guaranteed to survive. It was maintained. Open science makes this continuity explicit. It frames knowledge not as possession, but as something held in trust. What we produce is not only for our contemporaries, but for those who will come after us. To contribute to knowledge is therefore to assume responsibility for its transmission. Open science is not a radical departure. It is the natural extension of a long human obligation.

A Timeline of accumulation

• 17000 BCE: Cave paintings, including those at Lascaux Cave • 3400 BCE: Invention of writing, cuneiform in Mesopotamia • 1440 CE: Gutenberg Printing Press enables large-scale reproduction of texts • 1665 CE: First scientific journal, Philosophical Transactions • 2000s CE: Internet era, emergence of open access and open data Human knowledge transmission has evolved from prehistoric markings to written language, from print to digital infrastructures. Each stage has expanded what can be preserved, shared, and built upon.

What will remain

Across this history, from cave walls to digital repositories, the pattern is consistent. Knowledge is recorded. Knowledge is preserved. Knowledge is shared. Knowledge is extended. Open science does not introduce this process, but it makes it visible. And in doing so, it raises a question that is no longer abstract. What will remain usable?
